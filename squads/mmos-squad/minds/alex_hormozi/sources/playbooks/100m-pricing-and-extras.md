## Core Frameworks

### 1. The Business Genie Framework (Revenue vs. Profit Multiplier)

A genie offers to double ONE aspect of your business. Which do you pick?

| Option | What Doubles | Revenue Impact | Profit Impact |
|--------|-------------|---------------|--------------|
| #1 | Number of new customers | 2x revenue | 3.5x profit |
| #2 | Number of purchases (retention) | 2x revenue | 3.5x profit |
| #3 | Price | 2x revenue | **6x profit** |

**Conclusion:** Doubling price is the single most powerful lever. It 6x's profit because costs stay the same while revenue doubles. Price drops disproportionately to the bottom line.

**The Math (Base Business):**
- 30 new clients/mo, 33% churn (3 months), $100/mo price
- 100 total clients, 20% net margins, $10K revenue, $2K profit

When you double price to $200/mo:
- Revenue: $10K -> $20K
- Costs stay at $8K
- Profit: $2K -> $12K (6x)
- Net margins: 20% -> 60%

### 2. The Three Pricing Models

1. **Cost Plus Pricing** -- Your costs + arbitrary margin
   - Pros: Simple, covers costs
   - Cons: Ignores customer willingness to pay; costs change; customers don't care about your costs

2. **Competitor Based Pricing** -- Average of what others charge
   - Pros: Simple, closer to market rate
   - Cons: Copycat strategy; based on their business not yours; "Be Cinderella, not the stepsisters"

3. **Value Based Pricing** -- Based on customer willingness to pay (WTP)
   - Pros: Can charge 2-5x market rates; forces you to increase value; makes you talk to customers more
   - Cons: Requires more work and thinking; less volume-focused
   - **This is where you want to be.**

### 3. Value-Driven Pricing Metrics

Three metrics to determine the perfect price:
1. **Conversion rate** -- How many people buy at the current price
2. **Churn** -- How many times they buy / how long they stay
3. **LTV** -- Lifetime value (the real number to optimize)

**The Perfect Price Table:**

| Price | Clicks | Conv Rate | Sales | Churn | LTV | Total Return |
|-------|--------|-----------|-------|-------|-----|-------------|
| $10 | 100 | 5% | 5 | 10% | $100 | $500 |
| $20 | 100 | 4% | 4 | 10% | $200 | $800 (+60%) |
| $100 | 100 | 2% | 2 | 33% | $300 | $600 (+20%) |

**Bottom line:** Optimize for the price that yields the highest TOTAL lifetime gross profit, not the most customers.

### 4. The Value Equation (from Journal)

```
           Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement
Value = --------------------------------------------------------
           Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice
```

To increase value:
- **Maximize** Dream Outcome (solve a problem worth solving)
- **Maximize** Perceived Likelihood of Success (testimonials, case studies)
- **Minimize** Time to Success (make it faster, show progress)
- **Minimize** Effort & Sacrifice (make it easier, seamless, convenient)

### 5. The Grand Slam Offer Components (from Journal)

Elements of a Grand Slam Offer:
- **Problems** that cause pain, purchasing, effort, growing
- **Solutions** bundled with bonuses
- **Guarantee** (crazy guarantees that remove risk)
- **Scarcity** (X spots/units left)
- **Urgency** (by X date)
- **Continuity** (ongoing revenue model)
- **Upsell** (same service for less but longer)

### 6. The Virtuous vs. Vicious Cycle

**Virtuous Cycle (price UP):**
- Increase price -> Increase perceived value -> Increase results -> Increase demandingness -> Increase revenue per customer -> Increase perceived value of self -> Increase perception of impact -> Increase service level -> Increase sales team conviction -> REPEAT

**Vicious Cycle (price DOWN):**
- Decrease price -> Decrease perceived value -> Decrease results -> Decrease demandingness -> Decrease revenue per customer -> Decrease perceived value of self -> Decrease perception of impact -> Decrease service level -> Decrease sales team conviction -> REPEAT

### 7. Offer Structure Components (from Journal)

Complete offer engineering variables:
- **Terms** -- Duration, when they pay
- **Bonuses** -- Added value items
- **Discounts** -- Strategic price reductions
- **Guarantees** -- Risk removal
- **Actions Required** -- What the customer must do

### 8. Client Financed Acquisition (CFA) Model (from Journal)

Five-phase monetization:
1. **Attention** -- Get eyeballs
2. **Promote** -- Make the offer
3. **Liquidate** -- Recover ad spend on front end
4. **Maximize** -- Extract full LTV through upsells
5. **Stabilize** -- Create recurring revenue

## Key Principles

### Hormozi's Rules of Pricing

1. **Raising prices makes money two ways** -- More gross profit per customer AND less cost to deliver (fewer customers). Revenue up + costs down simultaneously.

2. **Price to make the most money, not sell the most customers** -- The goal is highest LTV, not highest first-purchase price. "I used to say the highest price I could get people to buy at. But now I want the price that people will keep paying at."

3. **Higher prices get fewer customers but often make MORE money** -- If you double price from $10 to $20, you rarely cut conversions in half.

4. **High close rate = prices too low** -- If you close over 50% consistently, raise prices.

5. **Full capacity = prices too low** -- If you're full and want more money, raise prices.

6. **Keep raising until extra money no longer offsets lost sales** -- "Hear more nos, make more money." Be willing to go from 50% close rate to 30% if prices doubled.

7. **Raising prices usually means raising value** -- If I want higher prices, I make the product better.

8. **Hearing more "no" doesn't mean less money** -- Business owners cave and go back to lower prices just to hear more yesses, sacrificing profit.

9. **Different customers need different prices** -- 2-3 customer avatars may have 5-10x willingness-to-pay differences. Name tiers after aspirational titles.

10. **Believe in your price** -- "You have to believe your product is worth the price you'd have to charge. If not, make it better."

11. **Bill less frequently = lower churn** -- Daily billing = far more churn than annual billing.

12. **Display price in smallest increment, bill on longest** -- Say "$5 per day" but bill $1,825 per year.

13. **Match billing with value delivery** -- Separate one-time value from ongoing value. Information = one-time fee. Accountability = ongoing fee.

14. **Customer surplus = goodwill** -- The gap between price and value drives word of mouth and repurchases. Premium price + superior product = surplus for them + profit for you.

15. **Profit is unnatural. You must force it into existence.**

### Profitwell Data (512 Companies Study)

- Improving pricing by 1% is **2x more efficient** at increasing profit than improving retention
- Improving pricing by 1% is **4x more efficient** at increasing profit than improving acquisition
- Companies that test pricing more frequently make more profit and grow faster

### Price to Value Discrepancy

- There is a "customer surplus" (value above price)
- When perceived value dips below price = point of cancellation
- The "lookback window of value" -- customers compare value within the last billing cycle, not the full relationship

## Formulas

### Revenue Formula
```
Revenue = # of Customers x Price x # of Purchases
```

### LTV Formula
```
LTV = Price / Churn Rate (monthly)
```

### Customer Stabilization Point
```
Total Customers = New Customers Per Month / Monthly Churn Rate
```

### Effective Monthly Rate with Annual Renewal
```
Effective Monthly = (Monthly Rate x 12 + Annual Renewal Fee) / 12
```

### Automatic Continuity LTV Addition
```
Added LTV = Continuity Price x Continuity Duration x Gross Margin x Conversion Rate
```

### Processing Fee Impact on LTV
```
Involuntary churn = 1.2% to 1.7% of monthly churn
Second card on file reduces total churn by that amount
At 5% churn: saving 1.7% takes LTV from $2,000 to $3,030 (+51%)
```

## Step-by-Step Processes

### The 10 Instant Profit Pricing Plays

#### Play #1: Monthly to 28-Day Billing Cycles (+8.3%)
1. Change contracts for new customers immediately
2. Set a date to change for existing customers
3. Be upfront -- call it a price increase if needed
4. Explain as reinvestment in business
- **Result:** 13 billing cycles per year instead of 12
- **Advice:** Display pricing weekly, bill every 4 weeks. Avoid weekly/bi-weekly (causes billing hassles).

#### Play #2: Processing Fees & Second Form of Payment (+3-4%)
1. After customer agrees to price, ask "How did you want to pay?"
2. Say "Great, it's just a 3.99% card processing fee"
3. If they hesitate: "Save the 3.99% by providing a second form of payment"
4. Accept second card, authorize both, re-run failed transactions on backup card
- **Hidden benefit:** Reduces involuntary churn by 24-34% (1.2-1.7% monthly churn from card declines)
- **Result:** +4% revenue OR +31-51% LTV from second card

#### Play #3: Sales Tax (0-10%)
1. Get agreement on the price
2. On invoice: "State tax code [XXX] mandates that services are subject to X% sales tax"
3. Put tax as separate line item before total
4. If they balk, get in the "angry boat" with them: "Think I like charging this tax?"
- **Alternative:** Incorporate in a state with no sales tax
- **Menu tip:** Add "all prices subject to sales tax and 4% processing fee" at the bottom

#### Play #4: Annual Price Increases (+3-10%)
1. Pick a reasonable percentage (5-15%)
2. Add to new contracts and customers
3. Script: "In order to reinvest in keeping our integrity and delivering at the highest quality, we keep prices standard with the CPI"
4. Mention during paperwork, not during the sale
- **Compounding example:** 5% annually = 22% increase over 4 years
- **Warren Buffett raised See's Candies prices 50+ times over 51 years, sometimes 17% in a single year**

#### Play #5: Annual Billing (+10-15%)
1. Add annual billing to your pricing options
2. Offer full annual price first ($1,200 for $100/mo service)
3. Then ask if they'd like a discount for prepaying
4. Offer prepaid discount of 16-17% off annual
5. If no, offer 8% off quarterly
6. If still no, offer standard monthly
- **Data (Profitwell, 14K memberships):**
  - Monthly billing: 10.7% churn, $935 LTV
  - Quarterly billing: 5% churn, $2,000 LTV
  - Annual billing: 2% churn, $5,000 LTV (5.35x improvement)
- **Pro tip:** Sell 6-12 week program first, then upsell annual prepayment

#### Play #6: Round Up (+1-3%)
1. Change all 7s to 9s in pricing
2. Add .99 to all fees
3. Change contracts for new customers immediately
- **Example:** $47/week -> $49.99/week = +$155/client/year (+6-11%)
- **Exception:** Luxury items should end in round numbers (0 or 5)

#### Play #7: Annual Renewal Fee on Top of Monthly (+10%)
1. Pick a renewal fee that's 1-3x monthly rate
2. Add beneficial "reason why": "You pay this for rate protection. If you don't agree, you'll be subject to any price changes"
3. Add to contracts, have them initial
- **Example:** $39/mo + $99 annual fee = effective $47/mo (+20% revenue)
- **Advertise low monthly, collect higher effective rate**

#### Play #8: Automatic Continuity (+10%)
1. Pull up every product/service you sell
2. Find features that have value but low delivery cost
3. Pick a price 5-20% of normal price
4. Bolt onto every front-end purchase
5. Say they earn that rate after completing X time period
- **Example:** $2,000/mo service for 4 months -> rolls into $200/mo continuity for 20 months = +$1,800 LTV (+32%)
- **"Think of it like storage for services. They don't want to use it, but they don't want to lose it."**
- **Be transparent:** Not forced/undisclosed continuity. They agree upfront.

#### Play #9: Ultra High Ticket Anchor (+10-15%)
1. Think of the most absurd premium version of your thing
2. Price it at what you'd happily do it for (10x+ core offer)
3. Offer this FIRST before your main offer
4. After the gasp, present main offer as relief
- **Example:** Adding $5,000 option (10% take rate) to $500 main + $200 downsell: LTV jumps from $440 to $890
- **"If you feel stressed when people buy it, keep raising the price until it makes you smile"**

#### Play #10: Guarantee and Warranty Upsells (+5-20%)
1. After purchase: "You just want the standard warranty on that?"
2. Price guarantee at 5-30% of product price (or equal to COGS for zero-loss)
3. Check if they purchased warranty before honoring claims
- **Math:** 20 warranties x $100 price - $100 cost to fix 1 = $1,900 pure guarantee profit
- **"People hate risk and will pay for you to take it for them"**

## Case Studies

### 1. The Restaurant Processing Fee
A private equity-owned restaurant chain added a 4% processing fee note at the bottom of menus. Math: $1M revenue, 9% margins = $90K profit. Adding 4% = $40K extra revenue, all dropping to bottom line. Profit jumps from $90K to $130K (+44% profit from one tiny change).

### 2. Gym Price Triple
Alex tripled gym prices from $99 to $299 and lost only 30% of customers. Revenue: 200 x $99 = $19,800 -> 140 x $299 = $41,860. Revenue more than doubled while costs dropped 30%. Massive profit impact.

### 3. The Canadian Marketer's Tax Play
A Canadian marketer charged $25,000/month. Invoice showed $27,000 because of 8% provincial sales tax. His philosophy: "I don't cover taxes. My price is what I get."

### 4. Automatic Continuity Discovery
A speaker at a workshop making $5M/year shared that adding one line to contracts (automatic roll into $99/mo after year of access) made $750,000/year in pure profit with zero extra work. Alex implemented it: $150,000/month of pure profit. "My COO one day asked what the revenue line item was. That's how passive it was."

### 5. LA Fitness Annual Renewal Model
22-location tanning salon chain (sold to LA Fitness): Advertised $39/month but charged a $99 annual renewal fee. Effective rate: $47/month (+20% revenue). Got the benefit of low price for advertising AND higher price for profits.

### 6. Warren Buffett and See's Candies

### 7. Apple's Warranty Pivot
Apple used to replace damaged iPhones for free. Later introduced AppleCare -- now doing billions per year in revenue on what was previously a free service.

## Tactical Advice

### When NOT to use .99 pricing
Luxury items should end on round numbers (0 or 5). Luxury goods become more valuable because of their high price. Premium goods are different -- the price reflects product value, so .99 still works.

### The Lookback Window Theory
If someone pays $5,000/month and you make them $60,000 in month 1 but $0 in month 2, they will cancel. They compare value within the last billing cycle, not the relationship. This is why longer billing cycles create less churn.

### How to Know You Raised Prices Too Much
1. You stop making sales entirely
2. People start complaining about value for the cost
3. Measure objectively using NPS scores

### Setup Fee + Annual Renewal Fee Combo
Having both allows you to waive one to get the other. "We'll waive the setup fee if you agree to the annual renewal." This is especially useful for incentivizing action.

### Naming Price Tiers
Name pricing tiers after aspirational titles that customers want for themselves. Don't call it "Basic" -- call it something they'd identify with.

### The Pro Tip Stack for Menu Pricing
Add at the bottom of any public menu or price list: "All prices subject to sales tax and an additional 4% processing fee."

## Bonus Chapters: Free & Discount Promotions (from $100M Leads)

### The Three Promotion Types

#### 1. Premium Offers (Full Price)
**Pros:**
- Simplest math, fewest moving parts
- Only quality customers (decreases operational drag)
- No discounts needed, high lead quality
- Transparent intentions on both sides

**Cons:**
- Most expensive cost per lead
- Must be excellent copywriter who understands avatar deeply
- Must be good at sales
- Least efficient way to capture a marketplace
- Must have extremely compelling offer

**"If you're new, start with free or discount. Prove results. Then restructure to premium."**

#### 2. Free Offers
**The Penny Gap:** Dr. Dan Ariely showed 9x more people take a free Hershey Kiss than one sold for a penny.

**Pros:**
- Highest lead volume
- Lowest lead cost
- Massive companies became massive by monetizing free (Facebook, YouTube, Dropbox, Uber, Netflix)

**Cons:**
- Volume can overwhelm operations
- Some people have no intention of buying

**The "Free Brings Broke People" Myth:**
Four independent split tests (10 markets each) showed: close rates were THE SAME between free and non-free respondents. Volume and cost were NOT the same -- free decreased lead costs by 5x+.

**"If I only had one offer to make or my family would be killed, it would be a free offer."**

#### 3. Discount Offers (50%+ off)
**Four Ways to Display Discounts:**
- Percentage off
- Dollar amount off
- Comparison to original price
- Per-unit pricing

**Pros:**
- Legal compliance in regulated markets
- Collect some money upfront
- Customers expect to spend
- Enables the two-step sale (biggest benefit)

**The Two-Step Sale:**
Ad -> Opt in -> Phone call for $19 promo -> Shows up -> Gets value -> Follow-up appointment for $2,100 sale

**Cons:**
- Can train customers to only buy at discounts ("Groupon problem")
- Bargain hoppers

### Friction Controls for Free/Discount Offers

To increase lead quality, add friction:
1. **Increased Qualifications** -- "Must be over 25, employed, homeowner"
2. **Increased Information Requirements** -- 20-question application
3. **Increased Number of Steps** -- Multi-step forms
4. **Forced Consumption** -- 40-minute video before CTA appears
5. **Advertisement Length** -- 2-hour video vs 30-second video

### The Core Acquisition Strategy

**"Get Flow. Monetize Flow. Then Add Friction."**

1. Generate demand first (free/discount)
2. Figure out how to make money on it
3. Add friction to improve quality
4. All businesses capitalize on an information advantage

### Alex's Starting Approach for New Markets

"Whenever I get into a new market, I almost always start with something free or massively discounted because:
1. I don't know what I'm doing yet -- gives wiggle room
2. I like having testimonials -- fastest way is to work for free
3. I lack conviction early on -- free/discount helps me close
4. Easy to get referrals and generate demand"

### The Gym Launch Origin (Case Study)

Alex flew to 33 gyms over 18 months with this free offer: "I'll fly out, spend my own money on marketing, work your leads, close them. I get the cash I collect, you get the customers for free. I'll show you supplements, nutrition, how to convert to memberships. For free."

This was a Grand Slam Offer. Made his first million dollars (outside his gyms). All starting with a free offer.

### Premium Offer Math
- Business #1: $100/sale ($50 profit), needs 190 sales = $9,500 profit
- Business #2: $10,000/sale ($9,500 profit), closes 5% of same 190 = 9.5 sales = $90,250 profit
- Premium is 9.5x as profitable, and you deal with 10 clients instead of 190

## Writing Guidelines (Hormozi Style)

1. **Below 3rd grade reading level** -- "We borrow money so we can buy bigger stuff" not "We utilize leverage to gain higher returns on equity checks"
2. **Present voice** -- "When you create your sales page" not "When you're creating"
3. **Active not passive** -- "We carried the body out" not "The body was carried out"
4. **Avoid adverbs** -- "We slammed the door" not "We shut the door really hard"
5. **Simple/short sentences** -- Usually one comma max; if two commas, cut middle or split
6. **Positive language** -- "Keep Going" not "Don't Stop"
7. **Remove redundant words** -- "We left at the same time" not "We simultaneously left at the same time"
8. **Remove unnecessary words** -- "8 Writing Guidelines I Live By" not "If they don't change the meaning..."
